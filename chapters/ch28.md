# Chapter 28: The Hollow Shore, Mended

The ferry on a Tuesday morning in October held six passengers and a small white dog. Maya sat on the open deck in her Boston coat and watched the mainland pull away behind her, and the mist move across the surface of the bay the way it had moved a year ago, and the morning light land on Blackwood Island exactly the way it had landed the first time.

She had not been back since the Swiss extradition hearing.

Margaret Swift met her at the dock. Margaret, who had broken a twenty-year silence to lead them to the cave entrance, and who now wore the blue canvas jacket of the Children of the Hollow Shore Trust with the small sea-bird logo on the pocket. Margaret had stayed on as caretaker, on her own request. The board had offered her a pension and an exit. She had declined.

"Ms. Chen."

"Margaret."

"Construction's finished on the north wing. Workmen cleared out last week. The plasterers were having opinions about the ceiling medallions, but I told them what they were going to do."

"I bet you did."

They walked up the path together. The path Maya had walked a year and a month ago with Captain Murphy's voice in her ear and a fifty-thousand-dollar forged commission in her bag. The branches that had leaned over the path that October had been cleared. The path itself had been widened, a gentler slope for wheelchairs and small children. Halfway up, a new wooden bench sat under a stand of pines, and a small brass plate on the bench said *For Tommy Morrison, who gave his sandwich away.*

Maya sat on the bench for a second. Margaret waited.

"I was going to get this put up without telling Linda and Mark," Maya said. "I thought they might not want it public. They wrote me a letter telling me to stop second-guessing and put it up."

"They're on the ferry at noon. They said they'd come for the opening."

"Danny too?"

"Danny too. He's twelve now. He wants to see the bench."

"Good."

They kept walking. The house, as they came up to it, was not the house Maya remembered. It was painted a soft blue-gray instead of the Gothic tar-black of a year ago. The broken shutters had been repaired. The wraparound porch had been reinforced. A sign on the door that had once said *Blackwood House, est. 1890* now said *Children of the Hollow Shore, Trust Retreat & Archive.* A small copper bell hung beside the door. A handwritten index card was pinned below the bell: *Please ring, we sometimes have sessions.* The handwriting was Emma Washington's.

Inside, the entrance hall had been gutted. The portraits were gone. Maya had handled that personally. The walls were now hung with children's paintings and, at the far end, with a mounted display of a single chalk drawing, preserved under glass. The drawing was of two small figures beside a mermaid. An M. An S. A fierce line between them. Margaret had gone down into the caves last March with a conservator and a great deal of spray fixative to bring it up whole.

Maya stopped in front of it. She looked at the drawing for a long time.

"Dr. Chen called last week," Margaret said, after a while. "She wanted to know if you were coming up. I said I didn't know."

"I'm coming for the opening. And for. Something else."

"Your mother and father are on the ten o'clock."

"I know."

"They've been up twice this summer. They're. Finding their feet."

"I know."

Maya put her hand on the glass, once, lightly, and took it away.

"The news out of Geneva," Margaret said. "About Renault."

"He gets out at fifty-three. Eleven years, four months. He took the first plea and kept it."

"I saw the testimony from his sixth deposition. About the Alistair transfers. Patricia said it was the cleanest piece of witness work she'd ever put her name to."

"It was."

"You've spoken to him."

"Twice. Through the Bureau. Once to thank him. Once because we had a question about a name in a ledger. He answered the question."

"And the Bureau lets you."

"The Bureau has given up trying to decide what I am and is letting me be whatever it is I am."

Margaret smiled a small Maine smile. "That sounds about right."

They walked through the house. The library, where Eleanor had sat pouring a drink and a year ago had tried to plan a murder, was now a reading room. A young woman Maya did not know was unpacking a box of donated children's books onto a low shelf. She looked up and smiled at Maya and went back to her work. The dining room where Dr. Richard had asked about psychology programs was now an art studio with long tables and spattered dropcloths and a sink with sixteen tins of brushes beside it. Emma Washington was in the studio. She was showing three small girls how to pin a piece of paper to a board so the edges did not curl under the watercolor wash.

Emma looked up. She was fifteen now, serious-eyed, still small for her age. She was in a blue hoodie that said HOLLOW SHORE ART FAIR 2027. Her hair had grown out.

"Maya."

"Hey."

"You made it."

"I made it."

"Mom's with me. She brought lunch. She brought enough lunch for everyone on the island, because she doesn't know how to stop doing that yet."

"Then let's all eat."

Emma's hand stayed on the corner of the little girl's watercolor paper. Anchoring it while the girl worked.

"The Mermaid Room," Emma said. "I saw it."

"Yeah."

"It's the way I remember it."

"Yeah."

"Thank you."

Maya did not say anything. Emma did not need her to.

---

The ribbon-cutting was on the wraparound porch at eleven.

Maya had tried to get out of speaking. Patricia Valdez, who had come up from Portland on the eight o'clock ferry and was on the porch wearing a coat too heavy for the weather, had not let her get out of it. Dr. Sarah Chen was there, up from Portland on the same ferry, standing with her hands in her pockets and her dog at her knee, a medium-sized shepherd mix with one blue eye who was looking at Maya with the polite interest of a dog who has been told that this is a workday. Detective Lisa Park was on the porch in her state-police jacket with no weapon visible. Agent Sarah Martinez was in civilian clothes beside her. Agent Kim had brought his wife, who taught fifth-grade math in Gray and who had wanted to meet Emma Washington for eight months.

The Morrison family arrived at ten-forty-five on the noon ferry, which the Trust had pulled in early for the day. Danny Morrison was twelve. He had grown three inches since the cave. He was holding a small brass plaque wrapped in tissue paper. Linda was holding his other hand. Mark walked behind them carrying a cardboard box full of Xerox copies of documents from his twenty-three-year archive that he had been told, by Patricia, not to bring, and had brought anyway.

The Hendricks family was there from Vermont. Carol and Robert and Michael, who was twenty-eight now and who had driven with them the entire six hours in the back seat of Robert's old Subaru without asking to stop, which Carol had counted as a small victory and had told Maya about in a text message at six that morning. Michael was in a gray coat that did not quite fit him, sleeves slightly too short, and he was standing beside Emma at the edge of the porch in a quiet-together kind of way that did not require either of them to speak.

Eight other survivor families had come up. Eleven others had sent letters, which Patricia had stacked in a folder and would read aloud inside, after the ceremony, in the new reading room. Two families had come from Colombia, flown up by the Trust, to thank Maya for Ana, who had been pulled out of a compound outside Medellín in March on the strength of Emma Washington's hospital testimony a year earlier. A Jonas Müller had flown in from Hamburg with his mother, who spoke no English, who had kissed both of Maya's hands at the dock without saying anything.

Maya was asked to say ten words or ten minutes, her choice. She chose ten.

"This house took," she said, "and today it begins to give back."

She stepped away from the microphone. Danny Morrison came forward with his plaque. He unwrapped the tissue paper with careful twelve-year-old hands and held the brass up so Maya could read it.

*In memory of Tommy Morrison, who gave his sandwich away. / In memory of Sarah Blackwood, who wrote it down. / For every child who told, and every child who could not tell, and every child who is beginning to.*

Danny's voice did not shake. He had practiced.

"Thomas was my uncle," he said. "He was ten years old when he was killed. Sarah was my mom's best friend. She was thirty-three. Thomas didn't get to write it down. Sarah wrote some of it down. I am twelve, and I am writing the rest."

He handed the plaque to Linda. Linda handed it to the Trust director, who passed it to the carpenter waiting on the porch with his drill. The carpenter mounted the plaque beside the front door, under the copper bell. It took him ninety seconds. Nobody moved while he did it. When he was done he nodded at Danny Morrison, and Danny Morrison nodded back, and Linda Morrison put her face in Mark's shoulder for a moment and then straightened up again.

Patricia Valdez spoke next, because the ceremony had to have at least one lawyer. She spoke for four minutes about the ongoing prosecutions, about the eleven trials still to come, about the Trust's intake schedule, about the ways survivors could reach them confidentially. She did not say her own name. She did not thank Maya. She had been told, firmly, at seven that morning, that she was not to thank Maya. She thanked the Morrisons instead, and the Hendricks family, and the eighteen cave survivors by name, and she thanked Detective Park for having been willing to reopen Sarah Blackwood's drowning three years earlier than most jurisdictions would have. When she finished nobody clapped. Clapping had not seemed right. People stood in the autumn sun on the porch and looked at each other.

Dr. Sarah Chen came up to Maya afterward, the shepherd mix padding beside her.

"Ten words."

"I had to. The others were not steady. Somebody had to be the one who said almost nothing."

"You did that well."

"Thank you for coming up."

"I was going to come up whether you asked me to or not."

"I know."

"The mother call."

"I did it. In March. I asked the one question."

"And."

"She said yes. He had. Three times. Once when I was nineteen. Once when I was twenty-four. Once when I was thirty-one. She said she did not answer after the first time. She said my father did not know. She said she was telling me now because she had been carrying it for thirty-one years and it was mine."

"Maya."

"I know."

"How did you respond."

"I thanked her for telling me. I said it was the answer I had expected. I asked her to tell my father in her own time. I said goodbye. I sat in my car for forty-five minutes."

"Good."

"I still do not know what to do with it."

"You do not have to know what to do with it. You only have to know that it is true. The work on what to do with it will take years. You have years."

"Thank you, Sarah."

"Save the thanks. Come have a stew later. Margaret is making a stew."

"I heard."

Dr. Chen's dog nosed Maya's hand. Maya knelt briefly and scratched behind the ears. The dog accepted this with professional composure and then trotted back to Dr. Chen, and the two of them walked off down the porch toward the reading room, where Patricia was already reading the first letter aloud.

---

Her parents came up on the noon ferry.

They were older than they had been last year. This was simple arithmetic and it was also something else. Maya had seen them twice in the year since she had made the phone call from the Portland hotel, and both of those visits had been in Columbia, South Carolina, in the house she had grown up in, at tables she knew. This was the first time she had seen them stand on the dock at Blackwood Island.

Her father got off the ferry first. He was carrying Maya's mother's overnight bag, in addition to his own. His hair had gone fully white. He was sixty-seven and he moved like a man who was older than he was and trying not to look it.

Her mother walked behind him, looking at the path up to the house. She stopped at the bottom of it. She did not cry. She did not say anything. She just stood there and looked up.

Maya walked down the dock and took her mother's small bag without being asked.

"Mom."

"Maya."

"Let me show you the bench first."

"The bench?"

"Halfway up. For Tommy. I want you to see it before we go up to the house."

Her mother's mouth went flat the way her mouth went flat when she was holding something back she did not yet want to set down. She nodded.

They walked up to the bench. Maya sat on one side. Her mother sat on the other. Her father stood, because he could not sit on benches anymore without his knee, and held the bags.

Her mother read the brass plate. She read it twice.

"We didn't know his name, Maya. All those years. We couldn't let ourselves know it. Your father and I. We let ourselves know there had been a boy. We let ourselves know something had happened. We did not let ourselves know his name, because knowing his name would have meant." She stopped. "You see."

"I see."

"He had a name."

"He did."

"Tommy Morrison."

"Yes."

Her mother put her hand on the brass plate. She did not say anything else for a long time.

Her father cleared his throat once. Maya looked up.

"Emma Washington has a drawing up on the wall," he said. His voice was smaller than it had been a year ago. "Margaret told me on the phone. I'd like to see it."

"Dad."

"I want to see it, Maya. I know I'm not entitled to see it. I want to see it anyway."

"You can see it."

"Thank you."

They went up the rest of the path slowly. Her mother's hand on Maya's arm. Her father three steps behind, with both bags, a man who had decided to carry what he could carry for as many days as he was given.

---

In the late afternoon, after the lunch, after the ribbon-cutting, after the small awkward speeches and the photograph for the local paper and the ferry back to the mainland for the reporter, Maya walked down to the small rocky beach where the path ended.

The hollow shore.

She walked out onto the gravel. The tide was down. The flat stones the waves had been polishing for a century were laid out in a bright wet line along the surf.

Her parents came down a few minutes later. They stopped where the gravel started. Her father cleared his throat but did not speak.

"Come here, Dad."

"Your mother's knee."

"I'll come help."

Her mother took Maya's hand and went carefully down the slope. The three of them stood together at the edge of the water. The afternoon sun was warm. The sea was a calm brilliant blue.

"You're going to teach me," her father said.

"Yeah."

"I'm going to be bad at it."

"Yeah."

"I have not tried to skip a stone since I was nine."

"It's fine. Watch."

Maya found a flat stone. Gray, palm-sized, cool. She weighed it in her right hand. Her finger, on pure reflex, tapped once against the smooth surface. She did not try to stop it.

"The flat ones," she said. "Tommy told a friend of mine that once. The flat ones."

She threw.

The stone bounced once. Twice. Three times. Then sank.

Her mother made a small sound.

Maya put another flat stone in her father's hand. She folded his fingers over it.

"Sidearm. Flat on the water."

Her father did not say anything. He set his feet. He threw.

The stone skipped.
