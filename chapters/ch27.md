# Chapter 27: The Ghost's Gambit

The safe house the Swiss gave them sat three floors above the Rhône and had a window that did not open. Maya set the briefcase on the low table beside the window and did not let go of it. The Geneva federal prosecutor's courier would arrive in fourteen minutes, and she was not taking her hand off the briefcase before that.

"Kim."

"I'm here." His voice came through the secure laptop on the dining table, thin and tired and nowhere. "Martinez said you walked out with an evidence case. Tell me what is in it."

"Certified copies of Fairchild's personal financial records going back thirty-two years. Every payment to Dr. Blackwood. Every transfer to Mr. Alistair. Every wire to the Nightingale Fund. Guest lists from every ritual weekend at the Wyoming estate. Ritual calendars. Membership ledgers for what they called the Circle. Seventeen names I do not know yet. Fifty-two I do. A sworn, notarized statement from Ethan Renault. The originals are in a Zurich bank vault with release instructions to the Department of Justice contingent on his cooperation, which, given he is currently in Swiss federal custody a kilometer from where I'm standing, is not in doubt."

The laptop was silent for a second.

"Maya."

"Yeah."

"If this is clean paper, that is the prosecution of the decade."

"It is clean paper."

"Jesus."

"Kim, listen. I need you to start warming up a task force. We are going to lose a window if the Circle's people hear about the arrest before the indictments are sealed. They will move money. They will move themselves. They will move children if there are any still moving, and there might be. You have four hours. Maybe six."

"Martinez is three steps ahead of you. She's been on the phone to Washington since we got off the Swiss perimeter. They've already got the task force spun up. Treasury is lining up asset seizures in eleven jurisdictions. Interpol is prepositioning. Europol is prepositioning. We were waiting on confirmation that the evidence existed, which apparently now you are, plus a physical briefcase."

"Tell them the briefcase is in Swiss federal prosecutorial custody in nineteen minutes. A Special Agent in Charge is going to sign for it."

"How are you holding up."

"I am holding up fine, Kim. Start the clocks."

She heard him type something. She heard him stop. She heard him type again.

"Okay," he said. "Clocks are running. Kick-off time is oh-four-hundred Geneva, which is oh-five-hundred Bucharest, oh-four-hundred London, eleven-hundred local in Tokyo, and twenty-two-hundred yesterday in the Caymans. Simultaneous entry. They went with the predawn envelope because Fairchild's lieutenants travel badly in the morning."

Maya watched the Rhône through the sealed window. The water looked mercury-colored under the streetlights.

The doorbell of the safe house rang at 22:56. The Swiss federal prosecutor's courier and the U.S. Special Agent in Charge for this case stood in the hallway with two more armed men behind them. Martinez watched Maya's face for one second and then opened the door. The SAC signed for the briefcase in triplicate. The Swiss prosecutor signed his side. A chain-of-custody form got stapled to the evidence tag. The briefcase left the room. It was in the prosecutor's car at 23:02, and in a diplomatic-status pouch at Geneva Airport's secure cargo area at 23:34, and on a U.S. government plane by 00:15.

Maya made herself stop looking at her watch.

"Tea," Martinez said. "There's tea."

"I don't want tea."

"You are going to drink tea or you are going to fall asleep standing up. Pick."

Maya drank the tea.

The raids went at four in the morning Geneva time.

Kim fed the feeds from Portland. Maya and Martinez sat in a borrowed conference room at the U.S. Consulate, watching four monitors. Each monitor carried a tactical feed from a different continent. They were watching without listening, because the audio was classified at a level Maya did not have and Martinez had stopped caring about.

London. A townhouse on Eaton Square. Two plainclothes officers walking up to the front door with a search warrant while armed teams came over the back garden wall. The door opening. A man in a silk bathrobe. Handcuffs.

Bucharest. The house of Mr. Alistair. The same house that had been on Eleanor Blackwood's hand-drawn map from the interrogation three weeks ago. Romanian special police coming through the gate. A man in his sixties opening the door in pajamas. A smaller man beside him reaching for something inside the front hall closet. One of the Romanian officers moving faster than him. No shot fired. Both in custody.

Grand Cayman. A small office park. A lawyer's office with the name MCLELLAN & PARTNERS on the door. Two Cayman officers and an American forensic accountant walking in with a warrant and a truck. The lawyer protesting in a hallway. Files already going into boxes.

Wyoming. The Fairchild estate had been in federal custody since Chapter 19, but there was a separate facility Ethan had flagged. A ranch property sixty miles north of the main estate. FBI coming in at dawn. An empty house. Eight freezer chests in a basement room. Documents in the walls.

Maya watched Bucharest. She watched the smaller man's hand going for the coat closet in slow motion and watched the Romanian officer cross the floor in a single bright motion and watched them both go to the ground. She realized she was holding her breath only when Martinez elbowed her.

"Breathe."

"Yeah."

Tokyo. A high-rise apartment. Japanese police and an American legal attaché. A woman in her forties sitting at a breakfast bar in front of an uneaten grapefruit. No resistance. She went with them holding a cup of coffee and did not put it down.

Paris. Another brownstone. A man walked into his kitchen in a suit and asked the officers if he had time to call his lawyer and was told yes he did. He called his lawyer. He sat down. He waited.

Nineteen properties. Four continents. Fifty-two named principals. Forty-seven of them detained in the first six hours. Three more by noon. Two left uncaught. One of those was already on a plane to a non-extradition country and would be sorted out later. One of them had gone out a back door in the two minutes the Paris team had taken to run the knock-and-announce, but they had his passport and his phone and his housekeeper.

At the end of the first wave, Martinez stood up and stretched her back and said, "I have to make a call. You want me to bring you a sandwich when I come back?"

"No."

"You have not eaten since dinner yesterday."

"I am not hungry, Sarah."

It was the first time Maya had used her first name. Martinez did not mention it. She went to get a sandwich.

Maya sat alone in the conference room with the Portland feed on the laptop beside her. Kim had muted his mic. He was watching the same monitors she was, from ten thousand kilometers away, a small digital shield. She could see his coffee cup on the edge of his desk. She could see his reflection in his screen. She could see him push his glasses up his nose.

Her phone buzzed.

A text from an unknown number. No routing. No metadata she could make sense of in the three seconds she looked at it.

*Thank you. / E.*

Maya looked at the message for a long time.

Then she opened the briefcase she had been given for the safe-house stay, the smaller one Martinez had packed for her flight home. Ethan's original briefcase was halfway to Washington. This one was just her overnight kit. In a side pocket she had not yet unpacked she found a small sealed envelope. Plain cream. Her name on the front in the same careful handwriting as the label on the wooden bird from twenty-five years ago.

She opened it.

Inside: a single folded sheet of archival paper. Six lines of ink.

> *Maya,*
> *You chose right. Thank you for letting them have their names back when they want them.*
> *I meant everything I said tonight. I will keep my end. Twelve years. I will not run.*
> *Tell Emma Washington her mermaid on the Cathedral wall was still there when I went back last year. I made sure nobody painted over it. She may want to see it someday.*
> *Ethan*

Maya read it twice. She folded it again. She put it back in the envelope.

The text from the unknown number was presumably a relay. Ethan had been in custody for six hours when it sent. Somebody else had pushed the button. Maya thought about that for about thirty seconds and then stopped thinking about it, because it did not matter. The letter was the point.

Martinez came back with a sandwich Maya had not ordered and a cup of coffee Maya had not asked for, and Maya ate half of one and drank half of the other. On the monitors, the news cycle was starting. A BBC anchor looking slightly confused as the first press release landed on her desk. A Reuters banner. A New York Times push notification. A livestream from outside the Paris brownstone with police tape still up and a small crowd of neighbors in nightclothes being held back at the curb.

By seven in the morning Geneva time, the names were in the air. The Collectors' Circle had been built on silence for forty years. In the space of three hours it had become every wire service in the world.

At eight, Kim came off mute. His voice was hoarse.

"Maya."

"Yeah."

"We got them."

"Yeah."

"All of them, or enough of them that the rest will be crawling out from under rocks for the next ten years. The Trust paperwork Patricia sent through at midnight is already moving. The Justice Department is going to fight you on the medical files. You're going to win. Patricia says two years, maybe three. Then it's a functioning trust with the survivors as the beneficiaries."

"Good."

"You okay?"

"No." Maya drank the rest of the coffee. "But I'm going to be."

"Yeah."

"Kim."

"Yeah."

"Send Emma Washington a note. Nothing official. Just a card. Tell her we're not done, but tonight's the part where we won."

"I will."

Maya set the phone down on the conference room table and looked out the window. The morning sun was on the Rhône. Geneva was waking up. Somewhere a few kilometers away, in Swiss federal custody, a man named Ethan Renault was sitting in a holding cell waiting for an extradition hearing he had already agreed to, and somewhere a little further away than that, on the coast of Maine, a facility that was only a set of blueprints in a lawyer's office right now would, in another year, be a place where a woman named Emma Washington painted mermaids with a dozen children who had all been told their names were their own.

Maya put her right index finger flat on the table in front of her.

She did not tap.

She just let it rest there.
