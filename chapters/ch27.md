# Chapter 27: The Orchid Room

Geneva at night smelled of snow and diesel. The city ran on jeweled movements, every bus on time, every shop window lit to museum specification, and the Rothschild Botanical Garden sat at its edge like a glass cathedral that had chosen not to participate.

Maya and Agent Martinez were met at the service entrance by a grim-faced commander of a Swiss tactical unit. There was no raid. No forced entry. They were guests. A cryptic email to Martinez's secure inbox had laid out the terms three days ago: *The curator of the Orchid Room will receive Ms. Chen at 10 p.m. She may bring one associate. Your cooperation is appreciated.* It was an invitation, not a threat, and the underlying menace was unmistakable.

"You have one hour," the Swiss commander said. His English was careful. "At twenty-three-hundred my men come in whether you signal or not. He does not leave this building."

"Understood."

"The Geneva federal prosecutor is standing by. He is also unhappy."

"Understood."

The Swiss commander's jaw worked. He nodded once and let them through.

They were led through silent, darkened corridors of glass, the silhouettes of exotic plants pressing in on them from all sides. The air was thick with the scent of damp earth and blooming things. Finally they reached a heavy, unmarked door.

"The Orchid Room. He is inside." The commander did not look at Maya. "We hold a perimeter."

The door hissed open on a space that stole Maya's breath. A vast, circular conservatory, its glass dome revealing a sky littered with cold, distant stars. Orchids everywhere. Thousands of them, in every conceivable shape and color, their petals like velvet, their patterns a riot of impossible beauty. The air was thick with their sweet, cloying scent.

In the center of the room, under a single soft spotlight, stood a man. Tall and slender, dressed in the simple, elegant clothes of a European academic. He had the same serious eyes as the boy in the 1998 school photograph. They were honed to a fine, intelligent point now, and they held a grief so long-practiced it had stopped asking to be seen.

"Hello, Maya," he said. His voice was calm, steady, with no trace of an accent she could place. The voice of a man who had spent a lifetime learning to be from nowhere. "I'm Ethan."

Martinez tensed beside her, weapon not drawn but not far from her hand. Maya shook her head once, small.

"You've been busy, Ethan," Maya said. Her finger tapped once against the phone in her pocket. Once, and then again, and then she made it stop.

A faint, sad smile touched his lips. "I had a lot of time to plan."

He gestured to the orchids around them. "Fairchild's passion. He believed that beauty could be captured. Preserved. Made to last." He paused at a Vanda with petals like bruised velvet. "This one he had bred for thirty years. He believed he was selecting for something like a soul."

"Was he?"

"It's a flower, Maya. It's a very expensive flower that will die when nobody waters it. The rest of his beliefs worked the same way." Ethan's eyes moved past her to the starlight on the dome. "He has been, for my entire adult life, a very rich man with a very specific madness. I am here to tell you what the madness was, because I do not think you have the whole of it yet."

"Then tell me."

Ethan began to walk. Slowly. The way a curator walks a visitor through a collection. Maya and Martinez followed.

"He believed," Ethan said, "that when his son died, Thomas's consciousness did not end. It scattered. He believed that if he could find the right children, at the right age, with the right neurological compatibility, he could gather those scattered pieces back together. Not into their bodies, but into one of them. A vessel."

"You believe this?"

"No. Of course not. There is no science for it. There has never been science for it. He hired researchers for forty years and none of them ever produced anything. There were men like Dr. Blackwood who took his money and ran a protocol that looked, on paper, like a serious research program, and in practice was a sedation schedule and a lot of medical paperwork. It was a ritual. The anchors in Wyoming are not in machines. He stopped pretending the science was real years ago. They are wooden boxes on a stone bench. They hold a toy, a photograph, a lock of hair. They are a chapel he built for his son."

"He spent his life and twenty-three children on a delusion."

"Yes."

"And you."

Ethan stopped walking. "And me."

Maya watched the side of his face. Under the spotlight it was a face she could almost fit to the boy's photograph, and not quite. Twenty-five years had done work here.

"He found me four months after I escaped the island," Ethan said. "I was in a children's home in Halifax. My family, such as I had one, had told the social worker a story that did not hold up. Fairchild's scouts watched for children who had bolted from unusual circumstances. I fit the profile. He flew me to Lake Geneva. He sat me in a room like this one and told me my brothers and sisters had been lost in a tragic accident and he was going to see to it I had every opportunity."

"He knew who you were."

"He knew exactly who I was. I was the one that got away. He loved that about me. He thought I was proof of something."

"What did you think?"

Ethan's hands were in his pockets. They stayed there.

"I thought I was the only person in the world who knew what Dr. Richard had done in that cave," he said. "I thought I was the only person in the world who could make it stop. I was twelve. I thought I had six months to live if I did not do exactly what I was told, and I thought I had the rest of my life to make it end. I chose the rest of my life."

"You became his curator."

"I became his everything. I managed this garden. I helped him plan his philanthropic foundations. I sat beside him at galas. I also, quietly, for twenty years, copied his files."

Maya stopped walking. Ethan stopped too.

"What did you copy?"

Ethan looked at her for a long moment.

"Come with me."

He led them off the main path of the conservatory, down a narrower walkway behind a hedge of Cymbidium, to a small service door recessed into the glass. He keyed a code. The door opened on a climate-controlled room the size of a small apartment.

File cabinets. Ceiling to floor. Four walls of them. Dark wood, brass handles. Neatly labeled. Boxes stacked on a long oak table in the center, the boxes also labeled, in handwriting that was the same handwriting as the label *Remember* carved into the back of a small wooden bird twenty-five years ago.

Two small rolling carts against one wall held archival storage boxes of a kind Maya had last seen at the National Archives. A microfilm reader sat in one corner. A large fireplace, Victorian, original to the building, sat in another. A stack of white paper packets waited on the mantel. A long-nosed lighter sat beside them.

"Paper," Maya said.

"Paper. Photographs. Bank records in triplicate. Every payment he made to Dr. Blackwood for thirty-two years. Every transfer to Mr. Alistair. Every wire to the Nightingale Fund. The guest lists from every ritual weekend at his Wyoming estate. The names of the other members. Twenty years of it. I started copying the first summer I understood what he was." Ethan's voice was level. "I needed somewhere to keep it where he would not think to look. He never came into the archive. He thought of it as boring. It was the safest room in his world."

Martinez, for once, did not speak. She was looking at the filing cabinets the way a woman looks at a bank vault she has been hunting for fifteen years.

"Sarah," Maya said. "Tell me about Sarah."

Ethan's calm finally broke. A flicker of something raw and painful crossed his face.

"Sarah was the one he had been waiting for. That is the phrase he used. He believed her neurology was compatible with Thomas's. He had Dr. Blackwood monitoring her from childhood. Testing her. The medications he gave her were supposed to keep her quiet until she was ready. She started remembering too soon. She started asking questions. Fairchild decided he could not wait any longer. Dr. Blackwood killed her on his instruction and made it look like drowning."

The scent of the orchids felt suffocating.

"He killed her for a ritual that was never going to work," Maya said.

"Yes."

"My God."

"I could not warn her. I did not know until it was done. I knew something was about to happen, but I did not know the schedule. I have thought about that for a year, Maya. I have thought about it every day." Ethan's voice stayed flat, but his eyes did not. "I am telling you this because I want you to know that I failed Sarah. Tommy I was too young to save. Sarah I was not."

Maya's hand moved to the inside pocket of her jacket and touched the small leather notebook she had been carrying from Portland all the way across the Atlantic. She did not take it out. She only put her palm over it through the fabric.

*I am going to call her. I have her number through a private investigator in Boston.*

Sarah had been three days from her own rescue when Richard Blackwood walked into her studio. Sarah had almost saved herself. Sarah had started the phone call Maya had ended up answering.

Maya did not say any of this out loud. Ethan did not need to be told.

Maya looked at him. She did not know what her face was doing. She did not try to control it.

"Maya," Martinez said, quietly, at her elbow. "Ask him what he wants from us."

Maya did not look at her. She kept her eyes on Ethan.

"What do you want from me, Ethan?"

Ethan gestured to the fireplace. To the neat stack of white paper packets on the mantel.

"I want you to tell me whether to burn it."

The spotlight in the Orchid Room had a small hum to it. Maya had not noticed until the room went this quiet.

"The financial records, the Circle membership, the ritual logs, the Wyoming guest lists, those are on the table. Those I can give to you. Those I want to give to you. Those will make the world a worse place for Fairchild's people for the rest of their lives."

Ethan's hand moved to the mantel. Rested on the top paper packet. The packet was thick.

"The files in my hand are medical records. They are the names of twenty-three children. They are photographs of them sedated. They are transcripts of therapy sessions conducted on them without consent while they were drugged. They are things Fairchild paid to have documented, because he believed the documentation mattered. They are evidence. They are also a weapon. If any of this reaches the open record, even in redacted form, every single one of those children becomes a public fact. Their real names attached to what was done to them. Their photographs on the internet forever."

Ethan's hand on the packet was steady.

"I can burn them. Right now. I have thirty-eight minutes before the Swiss open that door. The fireplace is drawing well. I have copies nowhere. This is the only set. If I put a match to this stack, those twenty-three children get to keep their privacy and some of their peace. Fairchild gets convicted on the financials alone. The network still collapses."

"And?"

"And we never know exactly what was done to whom. The survivors themselves will have fragments, the way you had fragments. They will never be able to reconstruct what was done because the record of what was done will no longer exist. Some of them will find that a mercy. Some of them will spend the rest of their lives asking questions nobody can answer, the way you would have, the way you did, for twenty-five years."

Maya stared at the packet on the mantel.

"Or I can give the packet to you," Ethan said. "And you can decide what to do with it. The other set of records, the financials, those are yours either way. But this."

He lifted the packet. Set it back down. A small flat sound on the wood.

"You are the only other person alive who knows what it feels like to be a child in these files. You are the only person in the world who gets to make this decision."

Maya's finger tapped once against the phone in her pocket. Then stilled.

"Fairchild," she said, quietly. "He took our pasts, Ethan. He stole our lives. Do we have the right to take theirs. Even to save them from being his evidence forever."

"That is the question."

"You have thought about it."

"For twenty years."

"What do you think."

Ethan looked at her. Whatever had settled behind his eyes did not move.

"I think I do not trust myself to decide," he said. "I have been loyal to his worst instincts for too long to be certain I am not still inside his shadow when I think about this. I think the only person in this building who has the moral standing to make this call is the person he tried hardest to erase."

The clock on the wall behind him read 22:28.

Maya stood in the Orchid Room, in a chapel of flowers bred by a rich man's delusion, and held the future of twenty-three stolen childhoods in the space of a single decision.

She did not speak yet.
