# Chapter 7: Aftermath

The police station in Bar Harbor felt like a different world from the gothic mansion on Blackwood Island. Fluorescent lights hummed overhead, casting everything in stark, honest light. Maya sat across from Detective Park and two state investigators, a digital recorder between them capturing every word of her statement.

"Take your time," Detective Park said gently. "I know this is difficult."

Maya looked down at her hands, still bearing small cuts from the brass letter opener. Twenty-four hours ago, she'd been a desperate private investigator running from her past. Now she was a witness in something she did not yet have the shape of.

"I don't remember everything," she said. "I want to be clear about that. Fragments, mostly. More every hour. I was on that island as a child, that much I'm sure of. My parents were working for the Blackwood Foundation. There were other children there. Something happened." She paused. "I don't have it all yet. I'm telling you what I have."

"Take whatever time it takes."

"The basement evidence is all there. Medical files for dozens of children going back twenty-five years, some marked deceased. My own file is in that set. There's a hidden room with active medical equipment, and we pulled a sedated child out of it yesterday. That's what I know for sure."

Detective Park nodded. "The boy we pulled. I have news on him."

"Is he all right?"

"Awake. Asking for his parents." Detective Park's expression softened. "Maya, there's something you need to know about him. His last name is Morrison."

Maya's breath caught. "Morrison."

"Danny Morrison. He's Tommy Morrison's nephew."

The name Tommy Morrison was not a surprise. It was a door opening onto a room she had been walking around all night without knowing it was there.

"Tommy," Maya said.

She gripped the edge of the table, tapped her right index finger against the laminate once, twice. Something small and old in her chest clicked into place. A boy. Ten years old. A sheet. Not a whole memory. But a weight that had a name now.

"The Morrison family has been looking for answers about Tommy's death for twenty-five years," Park said. "When they learned about Sarah Blackwood's suspicious suicide, they started investigating. They discovered your connection to the island through old therapy records."

"They hired me."

"That's what it appears. Through a lawyer. They knew if anyone could uncover the truth, it would be someone who had been there. Someone who might remember."

Maya felt a strange mix of emotions. Anger at being maneuvered. Also, under the anger, a quieter thing. Relief.

"We have one more item, and I want you to hear it from me." Park's voice was careful. "Dr. Richard Blackwood escaped yesterday. On the family yacht, shortly before we reached the dock. Coast Guard is tracking, but he's got a lot of coastline to hide behind. He is, as of this morning, a federal fugitive."

Maya closed her eyes. Of course he had run. The man who had run that house had been planning his exit for years.

"What about Eleanor?"

"In custody at the Bar Harbor station. Cooperating through her attorney, which means she is answering nothing without calculating it first. We have grounds to hold her on obstruction for what happened at the mansion while the arrest was being made. She will not walk free. She will also not talk."

"And James?"

"Giving his statement now. He's asked about witness protection. I've told him he can have it if his statement holds up."

Maya watched through the one-way glass as James sat in the interview room next door. He looked like he'd aged ten years in the past day. His hands shook as he accepted a cup of coffee.

"I want to tell you everything," James said, his voice small but steady. "Every detail I can remember. Every child who came to the island and didn't leave the same."

For the next three hours, James laid out a timeline. Children who had visited over the years. Some from wealthy families seeking Dr. Richard's "innovative therapy." Others, like Tommy, who had simply seen too much. James named names, provided dates, described the cover-ups that the family had performed and that he had allowed to happen.

"I enabled a monster for twenty years because I was too weak to face the truth," James said, tears streaming down his face. "Sarah died because I chose family loyalty over her safety. I can't undo that. But I can make sure it never happens again."

Maya watched him from the other side of the glass and found her own face wet.

Later that evening, she sat in the hospital room where Danny Morrison was recovering. He was small for ten, with dark hair. His parents, Linda and Mark Morrison, sat beside his bed.

"You're the lady who found me," Danny said when Maya entered. His voice was hoarse but clear.

"I am. How are you feeling?"

"Confused. The doctors say I was sick, but I don't remember being sick. I remember being at camp, and then nothing until I woke up here."

Linda Morrison reached for Maya's hand. "We can't thank you enough. When we lost Tommy, we thought we'd never get answers."

"How did you find me?" Maya asked.

Mark Morrison pulled out a folder. "It took years. Private detectives. Lawyers. Anyone who would listen. When Sarah Blackwood died, we knew it wasn't suicide. We started looking into her. That's when we found references to other children who had been on the island. Including you."

"Your therapy records from when you were eight," Linda added. "Dr. Richard had documented your treatment extensively. We realized you might be the key to understanding what really happened to Tommy."

Maya looked at the folder. Photocopies of documents she did not remember creating.

"How did you get access to Dr. Richard's therapy records? Those should have been sealed."

Mark and Linda exchanged a glance.

"Our lawyer, Patricia Valdez, has connections. She said she had sources who could help." Linda's eyes slid away. "We didn't ask too many questions. We were just grateful for the help."

Maya filed the evasion away. Her investigator's instincts were prickling. The forgery was good. Patricia was too well-sourced. Something about how cleanly the commission file had been built bothered her, and she did not yet have the word for it. She let it sit.

"We're sorry we deceived you," Linda said. "But we were desperate. Tommy was ten years old when he died. The same age as Danny is now."

Maya looked at Danny. "Your uncle Tommy was brave. He tried to protect other children. That's why Dr. Blackwood hurt him."

Danny nodded. "Mom showed me pictures of him. She said he would have been the best uncle ever."

"He would have been."

The next morning Maya found herself in a therapist's office in Portland. Dr. Sarah Chen, who had been careful to establish on the phone that she was no relation despite the shared surname, specialized in trauma recovery and memory integration.

"How are you processing everything that's happened?"

Maya considered the question. "I feel like I've been carrying a weight my whole life without knowing it, and now I can feel the shape of it. Not the whole thing. Just the shape."

"That's very common with recovered-memory cases. Your eight-year-old self and your adult self are trying to become the same person again, and that takes time. It doesn't happen in a week."

"I keep thinking about the other children. James mentioned at least six others who disappeared or changed dramatically after visiting the island. What if they're out there, living with suppressed memories like I was?"

"Almost certainly they are. The FBI has asked me to consult on the broader investigation. They will need experts in how this kind of memory interference looks."

"I want to help. I want to help find them."

"That's admirable. But you need to heal too. You can't pour from an empty cup."

Maya sat with that one. Then, because she had never been good at sitting still, she found a small notebook in her bag and wrote something down.

That afternoon she met with the Morrison family's lawyer, Patricia Valdez, in a conference room overlooking Portland's harbor.

"The FBI wants to offer you a consulting position," Patricia said. "Your unique perspective and your investigative background make you invaluable to this case."

"What would it involve?"

"Working with other families who suspect their children were victims. Helping identify patterns in Dr. Richard's patient files. Possibly testifying in federal court."

Maya thought about Danny. About Tommy. About Sarah. About all the children whose voices had been silenced.

"I'll do it. But I have conditions."

"Name them."

"Any other survivors we find get the best trauma therapy available, paid for by the investigation fund. And I want to be involved in developing protocols for memory recovery that prioritize the victims' wellbeing over the legal case."

"I think we can arrange that."

As Maya left the meeting her phone buzzed with a text from Detective Park: *Eleanor still stonewalling. Richard spotted near Portsmouth early this morning by a fishing boat, lost contact. Coast Guard expanding. James accepted WITSEC.*

Maya stood on the harbor pier, watching a ferry that ran to the outer islands. Somewhere out there, Blackwood Island sat empty now, its secrets partly exposed and partly still waiting.

She thought about the folder Mark Morrison had pulled from his briefcase. The commission file. The way Patricia Valdez had not quite looked at her when she'd said *connections*. There were other hands in this, and they were not the Morrisons' hands, and she did not yet have a name for whose they were.

The ferry horn sounded across the water. Maya watched it dock.

She stood there for a long time before she went back to her car.
