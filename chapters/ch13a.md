# Chapter 13½: James

The U.S. Marshals Service kept a safe house in a two-story colonial outside Augusta that had been a regional sales office for a lumber company until the lumber company failed in the recession of 2009. The Marshals had bought the building at auction. It was gray-shingled, unremarkable from the road, and sat on a gravel drive long enough that any visitor's car was visible for sixty seconds before the visitor reached the front door. Maya had been cleared to visit at exactly one time. She was allowed ninety minutes. A Marshal would sit in the kitchen.

James Blackwood was in the living room when she arrived. He had lost weight. His silver was coming in faster. He was wearing jeans and a plain flannel shirt and he had a paperback book open on the arm of the chair, which he set down when she came in without marking his page.

"Maya."

"James."

They stood for a second before they sat. Neither of them knew how to do this. A Marshal, a woman in her fifties with a gun on her hip and a coffee mug in her hand, nodded at Maya from the kitchen doorway and moved off down the hall. The house creaked the way old Maine houses creak.

"How is Danny."

Maya sat down on the couch. "Strong. Stronger every day. He has been home with Linda and Mark for three weeks. His room has the same posters it had when he was taken. He asked Linda if he could take them down. She cried for forty minutes and then she took them down with him."

"Good."

"Emma Washington is in her mother's house in Connecticut. She is drawing."

"Good."

"Michael Hendricks. The grown-up one. He is in a transitional program in Vermont. Carol sees him every day. He remembers the bookmark in his Hardy Boys book. Chapter four of *The Melted Coins*."

James's eyes closed for a moment. He pressed the heel of his hand against his mouth.

"I did not know about Michael," Maya said. "I did not think he was going to be one of the survivors. He was twenty-seven years old inside those caves. He has been in there half his life."

"Christ."

"He asked Carol, his first day back, whether she was still mad that he had gotten a D in math."

James laughed, a small cracked sound. It was not really a laugh. Maya waited for it to finish being whatever it was.

"How are you, James."

"Bored. Scared. Glad to still be alive. The Marshals will not tell me what the new name is going to be. I have stopped asking. They say I get the name after I finish a set of depositions. The depositions are going to take eight months. I should not say that to you either."

"Say whatever you want to say. I am a licensed investigator, not an assistant US attorney."

"Ha."

"James."

"Yeah."

"I wanted to come up here because I needed to tell you something in person. I have been trying to say it to a number of people in the last three weeks. Sarah is still the reason I am here."

James did not look up.

"I know what the reports say. I know what Patricia is going to argue in court. Sarah was the intended vessel. Fairchild's file called her the heir. I know what the medical records say. All of that is true and all of that is going to come out. But the thing that is true and is not in the file is that Sarah tried. She found the attic box when she was thirty-two. She started therapy with Dr. Torres. She started remembering. She started writing things down. Three days before she died she wrote in a notebook that she was going to call me. She had my number through a private investigator in Boston. She did not get to make the call."

James covered his face with both hands. He did not cry. He shook for a full minute and he did not cry. Maya waited.

"I had to," James said finally, his hands still over his face. "I had to know she was trying."

"She was trying. She did not know she was about to be killed. She was three days out from calling me. We were three days from her saving herself."

"Maya."

"Yes."

"Is this going to help you, or is it going to hurt you, if I give you something."

"Depends what you give me."

James stood up. He walked to a small knapsack the Marshals had let him bring, which sat beside the chair. He unzipped the smaller front pocket and he took out a leather-bound sketchbook the size of a paperback. He handed it to Maya. Maya did not open it yet.

"What is it."

"Her other journal. The one she did not keep at the house. I found it in a box in her Portland studio three days after she died. I did not show it to the police. I did not show it to you. I was afraid of what it said. I have read it four times in ten years. I have read it more carefully since the cave was opened. I am giving it to you. I am not taking it back."

"James."

"She wrote in it when she was eighteen and nineteen. After she had stopped being a kid and before she had started therapy. She wrote about the things she had started to remember by then. There are four pages about you, Maya. By name. She remembered you when she was eighteen. She was writing about you for fifteen years before she got around to calling you."

Maya held the sketchbook. The leather was very soft, worn in the way a thing gets worn from being held. She did not open it. Her right index finger tapped once against the cover and stilled.

"James."

"I should have given it to you three weeks ago. I should have given it to the police ten years ago. I know that. I am sorry. I am telling you now. You have ninety minutes left in this visit. If you want to read it here, I will sit with you. If you want to read it somewhere else, I understand."

"I am going to take it home."

"Okay."

"I am going to read it. And I am going to tell you what she said."

"Maya. You do not have to tell me."

"I am going to tell you. Because you deserve to know. And because Sarah would have wanted you to know. And because I am not going to let her last pieces sit in a sketchbook for another ten years without somebody reading them aloud to her father."

James's mouth worked.

"Okay," he said.

They sat for the rest of the ninety minutes. Maya did not open the sketchbook. James talked about the things he could say: the paperback he was reading, which was a John le Carré. The cat he was not allowed to get. The Marshal who had taught him how to make a decent chili. The way his accent had started slipping in the hours when he was alone, which nobody had warned him about, which he found more disorienting than anything else about the relocation.

When the ninety minutes were up, the Marshal came back into the living room with her coffee mug, empty now, and she said, "Ms. Chen."

Maya stood. James stood. They did not shake hands. Maya pulled him into a hug the way her mother would have hugged her at that age, if her mother had been the hugging kind, which her mother had not been. James held on for a second longer than Maya had expected. Then they let go.

"James."

"Yes."

"When the depositions are done. When they tell you what the new name is. Do not tell me. Do not send me a postcard with the new name. Send me a postcard with no return address, a postcard of whatever town you are in, a postcard with no words on the back. I will know it is from you. I will put it on my refrigerator. I will not try to find you. I will know that you are still alive and that you are still thinking about your daughter, and I will be able to keep doing this work."

James's eyes were full.

"Okay."

"Good luck, James."

"Good luck, Maya."

She walked out onto the gravel drive with the sketchbook pressed against her chest under her jacket. She got into her car. She did not open the sketchbook. She drove the hour back to Portland, parked in the garage under her own building, sat in the car with the engine off, and opened the sketchbook only when her hands had finally stopped shaking.

The first entry was dated April 4, 2011. It began: *I remember a girl named Maya. I do not remember her last name. I remember that her hand was very small, and warm, and that she had a birthmark shaped like a comma on the inside of her left wrist.*

Maya looked at the inside of her own left wrist. The birthmark was still there. Small. Shaped like a comma.

She read the rest of the sketchbook in her car in the parking garage until it was after midnight.

She called Dr. Chen in the morning. Not before. She wanted to carry it by herself for one night.
