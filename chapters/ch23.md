# Chapter 23: The Last Ghost

The memory came back all at once, in the FBI war room, at 4:12 on a Thursday afternoon, while Maya was standing in front of the evidence board holding a coffee cup she did not remember having picked up.

She set the coffee down on the edge of a desk. She said to Martinez, evenly, "I need an hour."

"Maya."

"I need an hour. I am fine. I need to be alone in a room for an hour."

"Okay."

She walked to her hotel. She did not remember the walk. She let herself into her room, set the chain on the door, sat on the edge of the bed, and did not move for a long time.

The memory was not a flashback. It was a reconstruction. Twenty-five years of sensory fragments assembling into their actual order, the way a shattered bowl assembles on a table when somebody has finally numbered the pieces. The gunshot in the cave. The sound of Tommy falling. The light going on. Sarah's hand in hers. A syringe. The smell of antiseptic. The blue wallpaper. The children's song about the crab. Ethan's face in the side tunnel for half a second before he vanished. A small wooden object pressed into her palm.

And her mother. Her mother's face at the back of the chamber, in the second the lights came on. Her mother's face registering what was in front of her and looking away. Looking away.

Maya put her hand over her mouth. She did not cry. She breathed.

After some minutes she said, out loud, to the empty hotel room: "She looked away."

Her hand went to her jacket pocket. The small wooden bird Ethan had pressed into her palm twenty-five years ago had been in the evidence box for three weeks and she had been carrying it in her pocket for two days for reasons she had not examined. She took it out now and held it in her hand. The carved wings pressed into her skin. *Remember.*

She did not know how long she sat there. Twenty minutes. An hour. The afternoon light had moved across the carpet once when she became aware of it again. Her phone had gone from missed-call state to voicemail state to message state. Dr. Chen had called twice. Martinez had called three times. Park had sent a text. A new message from Patricia Valdez sat at the top of the stack. The bird was in her fist and the fist had printed the shape of the bird into her palm.

She stood up slowly. She put the bird on the dresser. She went into the bathroom and she ran cold water on her face until the water no longer felt cold. She dialed Dr. Chen.

"Sarah."

"Maya. Where are you?"

"My hotel room in Portland. I had a memory event. Something between a flashback and a breakdown. I am past the worst of it."

"How long has it been."

"Ninety minutes."

"Are you alone."

"Yes."

"Can you eat."

"No."

"Are you thinking about hurting yourself."

"No."

"Good. Martinez has been trying to reach you. She is in the lobby of your hotel. I am going to call her and tell her to come up. I am going to come up too. I am in a car. I am forty-one minutes out on Route One. Do not be alone between now and when I get there. Tell me yes, Maya."

"Yes."

"Good. Open your door for Martinez when she knocks. Do not open it for anyone else without texting me first. I am coming."

"Sarah."

"Yes."

"I know everything now. The full memory. All of it."

"I know. I can hear it in your voice. That is the thing we have been working toward for a year. We are going to sit with it together. Not today. Today you just have to stay upright until I get there. All right."

"All right."

Dr. Chen hung up. Maya sat on the edge of the bed. She waited. She tapped her finger once against her knee and made it stop.

A firm, steady knock on her door four minutes later.

"Maya. Martinez."

She took a deep, shuddering breath and opened the door. Her face was pale, her eyes red-rimmed, but her expression was, when she looked up at Martinez, something more than recognizable. Something returning.

Martinez took in her appearance and the state of the room in a single, worried glance. "Are you okay?"

"I am now," she said, her voice flat. She held up the small wooden bird. "The summer of ghosts. Tommy. Sarah. All of it."

She told her. All of it. The summer of ghosts. The hollow shore. The escape attempt. The gunshot. And she told her about Ethan, who had been twelve that summer and who had pressed a wooden bird into her hand in the moment before he went into a side tunnel and vanished.

"Fairchild told me there was another one," Maya said. "In the Wyoming sitting room. He said the one who got away had been watching. He said I was going to meet him."

"Ethan."

"Ethan."

"And you think he hired you."

"I think he hired me through Eleanor's forged stationery. I think he has been working toward this for twenty years. I think he is why I walked onto that ferry in October."

Martinez sat with that for a second.

"Find him," she said.

The task was monumental. "It's a needle in a haystack the size of New England," Agent Kim said, staring at the board. "We're looking for a family that *didn't* become victims."

They had no last name, only a first name and an age from twenty-five years ago. They started with the list of families associated with the Blackwood Foundation that summer. They cross-referenced it with school records, property deeds, anything that could tie a family with a twelve-year-old son to the island. The work was slow, frustrating, a search for a shadow in a sea of shadows.

Maya, however, focused on the one piece of tangible evidence she had. "The bird," she said, handing the small carving to a forensics specialist. "It's driftwood, but the style is too clean, too specific. Find out where it comes from."

While Kim's team drowned in data, Maya held onto the physical world. Her right index finger tapped the edge of the table, slow and steady. "Look for the opposite," she told them, her mind working with a clarity born of her recovered pain. "Don't look for the families who stayed silent. Look for a family that fought back. Check for lawsuits against the Foundation, no matter how frivolous. He left a trail. I know he left a trail."

The forensics lab called first. A wood expert, a woman with a quiet, academic passion for her work, was on the line. "This isn't just whittling," she said, her voice excited. "This is a specific tradition of folk art carving. See the way the wings are shaped? That's classic Lunenburg style. It comes from a small, very specific community of artisans in Nova Scotia." She paused. "And there's a carver's mark on the tail. Tiny. Almost invisible. A stylized 'R.'"

Ten minutes later, a shout went up from Kim's corner of the room. He half rose from his chair, his voice cracking. "I've got a possible match. A family that threatened a lawsuit against the Blackwood Foundation for breach of contract, twenty-five years ago. They were on the island for one summer. The father was a shipping consultant from Nova Scotia. They left abruptly, just one week after the date we've established for Tommy Morrison's death. They vanished off the grid shortly after."

Martinez crossed the room in three strides. "What's the name, Kim?"

"Renault," Kim said, his eyes wide. "The son's name was Ethan."

Maya looked down at the wooden bird in her hand, turning it over until she found the tiny, stylized "R" on the underside of its tail. Her thumb brushed the mark once, then she set the bird carefully on the table, wings up, facing the corkboard.
