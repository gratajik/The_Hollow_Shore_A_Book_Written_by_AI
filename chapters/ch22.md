# Chapter 22: The Summer of Ghosts

**Blackwood Island, 25 Years Ago**

*The island, to eight-year-old Maya Chen, is magic. It smells of salt and pine and the damp, secret smell of moss in the deep woods. She arrives on a ferry that cuts through a silver fog, holding her mother's hand, her father beside them looking important with his leather briefcase. They are here for the whole summer, her parents hired as "therapeutic consultants" for the Blackwood Foundation's special summer program for gifted children.*

*Maya isn't gifted. She is just there.*

*She meets Sarah Blackwood on the first day. Sarah is small and quiet, with blonde hair that floats around her head like a halo. She has wide, serious eyes that see everything. They become friends with the fierce, instant loyalty of lonely children.*

*"They're always watching us," Sarah says on the second day, her voice low enough only Maya can hear. She tilts her chin toward the adults on the lawn. "Taking notes."*

*It's true. The adults (her parents, the Blackwoods, and especially Sarah's uncle, the charming Dr. Richard) are always observing, their smiles never quite reaching their eyes.*

*The two of them find the Cathedral on the third day, when Sarah slips out from under her grandmother's watch and pulls Maya by the wrist down the path past the carriage house. The cave entrance is small, a black mouth in the granite. Sarah has pocketed a piece of chalk from the Foundation's art room and a flashlight from her father's desk drawer, and she goes in first because she's braver than she looks.*

*The ceiling, when they find it, is twenty feet high. Sarah draws a mermaid on the wall in three quick lines. Maya laughs because it looks more like a spoon with hair. Sarah adds a crown.*

*"For you," Sarah says. "You're the mermaid queen."*

*"I don't want to be a queen," Maya says. "Queens have to stay on the throne. I want to be the one who goes into the woods and finds stuff."*

*"Okay. You're the scout."*

*"What are you?"*

*Sarah considers. She draws a second figure beside the mermaid, a small girl with a round crown. "I'm the one who stays. Somebody has to draw the map on the wall so you know where to come back to."*

*Maya thinks about this for a long time. It is the most serious thing anyone has ever said to her.*

*"I'll always come back," she says.*

*"Promise?"*

*"Promise."*

*Sarah draws an M next to the mermaid and an S next to the girl with the crown. She hands Maya the chalk, and Maya draws a small, fierce line between them. A string. A thing connecting the two.*

*Their world after that is the other children in the program. There are fifteen of them, all ages, all "special" in some way. Their leader, by unspoken agreement, is Tommy Morrison. He is ten, two years older than Maya and Sarah, and he is fearless. He has a gap-toothed grin and a charisma that pulls the other children to him. "Come on," he'll yell, "I found a new cave down by the shore!" and they all follow.*

*Then there is Ethan. He is twelve, the oldest of the children, and he is a ghost long before the summer is over. He is quiet, watchful, and he moves with a stillness that is almost polite. The adults call him another "special case," one who needs "quiet observation." He doesn't say much. When he does, the other children stop talking to hear him.*

*Their secret kingdom is the network of smuggler's caves that riddles the island's coastline. They call it the hollow shore. It is their sanctuary, a place away from the watchful eyes on the lawn. Tommy is the adventurer, pushing deeper into the darkness. Sarah is the artist, her chalk mermaids and sea monsters covering the Cathedral walls. Maya is the strategist, mapping their explorations in a small notebook she stole from her father's case. And Ethan is the scout, the silent one who notices things.*

*He is the first to notice the changes.*

*"They're putting lights in the deep caves," he tells Tommy one afternoon. His voice stays low. "And equipment. Medical stuff."*

*They start noticing other things. Children will be taken for "special sessions" with Dr. Richard and come back different. Quieter. Their eyes holding a look Maya doesn't have a word for yet. Years later, in a cave she has not yet set foot in as a child, she will see the same look in the eyes of rescued children and the word will finally come. Not now. Now she only knows that Jenny, who used to laugh at everything, stops laughing for two whole days after her session, and that David, who used to run, starts walking with his arms at his sides like a person doing a careful imitation of himself.*

*"He's drugging us," Ethan says to Tommy. They are huddled in the Cathedral, the one Sarah calls the Cathedral because of the high, vaulted ceiling. "In the juice at dinner. Small doses. To make us suggestible."*

*Maya doesn't know what suggestible means. She doesn't ask. She can tell from the way Tommy's face goes flat that it is a word she will understand later and not want to.*

*Tommy's adventurous grin is gone, replaced by a hard, determined line. "This isn't right," he says. "What he's doing to us. We have to get out of here."*

*"They watch the main dock," Ethan says. "They don't watch the old cove. There's a boat. I've seen it."*

*The game is over. The adventure has become a plan.*

*They move on a moonless night. Eleven of them, a small tribe of frightened children, led by their ten-year-old king and his silent, twelve-year-old advisor. Maya and Sarah hold hands in the middle of the pack, their hearts pounding a frantic rhythm against their ribs.*

*They move through the familiar darkness of the hollow shore, the sound of the waves a distant, promising roar. They are so close. The salty air of the hidden cove is on their faces.*

*Then the lights flash on, bright and blinding.*

*Dr. Richard stands at the mouth of the cave, his tall frame a terrifying silhouette. Beside him, Eleanor Blackwood watches, her face a mask of cold disappointment.*

*"Going somewhere, children?" Dr. Richard asks, his voice dangerously soft.*

*Tommy, brave, foolish, wonderful Tommy, pushes Maya and Sarah behind him. His voice cracks but holds. "You can't keep us here. We're leaving."*

*In that split second of confrontation, Ethan moves. He grabs Maya's hand and presses a small, smooth object into her palm. It is a bird, carved from driftwood. "Remember." The word is not a whisper. It is an order, pressed into her skin by his eyes. Then he is gone, folding himself into a side tunnel and vanishing the way he vanishes from rooms when adults enter them.*

*"Run!" Tommy's voice splits the cave.*

*Dr. Richard's charming face is gone. He moves with a speed that is shocking, pulling a small, silver handgun from his coat.*

*The gunshot is not loud. It is a flat, ugly pop that sucks the air out of the cave. Tommy Morrison falls, a small, crumpled heap on the sandy floor. A dark stain begins to spread around him.*

*The world stops. The children stand frozen.*

*The aftermath is cold and clinical. The children are herded back to the classroom chamber, the modified one with the medical equipment. Dr. Richard is calm again, his voice smooth and reassuring as he stands before them.*

*"This is what happens to children who don't follow the rules," he says, his eyes sweeping over their terrified faces. "But it's alright. You will all forget this. This was a bad dream."*

*Maya looks for her parents. She sees them standing at the back of the chamber, their faces pale, their eyes wide with a horror they do nothing to stop. Her mother looks at her, then quickly looks away. It is a betrayal so profound it steals the breath from her lungs.*

*A woman in a nurse's uniform comes toward her with a syringe. Maya's small hand tightens around the wooden bird in her pocket, the sharp edges digging into her palm. She looks at Sarah, who is crying silently. She looks at Tommy's empty space in their circle.*

*Then comes the prick of the needle. The world begins to swim, the edges of her vision going soft and fuzzy. The last thing she hears is the distant, rhythmic crash of the waves.*

*The waves on the hollow shore.*

*And then nothing.*

*When she wakes, it is a week later and she is in her own bed in Columbia, South Carolina, and the wooden bird is on her nightstand. Her mother does not explain how it got there. Her father does not mention it. Maya, who was told she had a bad fever at the Blackwood Foundation and had to be sent home early, does not remember packing it. She does not remember Ethan. She does not remember Tommy. She does not remember a cathedral with a mermaid drawn in chalk on the wall.*

*She does remember the bird is hers, the way children know a toy is theirs without being told. She puts it in the bottom drawer of her jewelry box, in the velvet slot meant for a ring, and she carries it with her from apartment to apartment for the next twenty-five years. A piece of driftwood she assumes she picked up on a beach somewhere, once, as a child.*

*She never throws it away.*
