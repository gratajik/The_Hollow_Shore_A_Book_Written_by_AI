# Chapter 6: The Choice

The phone kept ringing.

Maya stared at Dr. Richard, at the syringe in his hand, at the unconscious child on the bed. The IV line snaked from the boy's arm like a lifeline that could become a death sentence with one push of Dr. Richard's thumb.

"Choose, Maya," he said softly, his voice carrying the same clinical detachment he'd used once before, a quarter of a century ago, when his hands had been busy with a bundle in a sheet. The memory was not a picture. It was the smell of damp stone and the shape of a weight she'd been told never to name. "Answer that phone, and I increase the sedation. The boy won't wake up. Ever."

Eleanor stood behind him, her posture immaculate, her hands folded at her waist. "Richard." Two syllables, precise as enamel. "Do whatever is necessary."

James pressed himself against the wall, his face white. "Uncle Richard, please. He's just a child. Like Sarah was. Like they all were."

"Sentiment, James. Always your weakness." Dr. Richard's eyes never left Maya's face. "The phone, Maya. Last chance."

Maya looked at the boy. Maybe ten years old, dark hair falling across his pale forehead. His breathing was shallow but steady. Alive, for now.

The phone rang again.

"Detective Park," Maya said quietly, not moving toward the phone. Her right index finger tapped once, twice, against the brass letter opener in her palm. "She's been trying to reach me since yesterday. She knows something's wrong."

"Then she'll find your body on the cliffs tomorrow morning. Tragic suicide, brought on by recovered memories of childhood trauma." Dr. Richard adjusted his grip on the syringe. "The boy will simply disappear. Another runaway from the mainland. These things happen."

Maya felt the weight of the brass letter opener in her hand. Twenty-five years ago she'd been a terrified eight-year-old who'd made a promise to keep terrible secrets. But she wasn't that little girl anymore.

"You know what, Dr. Blackwood?" Maya's Southern drawl emerged strong and clear. "Some promises are meant to be broken."

She lunged forward, not toward Dr. Richard, but toward the IV line. Her fingers found the connection and yanked it free from the boy's arm just as Dr. Richard moved to inject more sedative into the bag.

"James. Help me."

For a moment, James froze. Then something shifted in his face. Twenty years of manipulation and fear cracking like ice in spring. He threw himself at his uncle, grabbing for the syringe.

"No more," James said, his voice breaking. "I let you drug my daughter into silence. I let you destroy her mind to protect family secrets. No more."

Dr. Richard stumbled backward, the syringe flying from his hand to shatter against the wall. "You fool. You're destroying everything."

Maya scooped up the unconscious boy, surprised by how light he was. Too light. How long had Dr. Richard been drugging him? "James. The passage. We need to get him out."

Eleanor moved to block their path, her face composed as a closed door. "Ms. Chen. You are overtired and overstimulated. Put the child down."

"Step aside, Eleanor."

"You don't understand what you are disturbing."

"I understand fine." Maya's drawl stayed on the words, thick and low. "Step aside."

Eleanor did not move. Maya shouldered past her. The matriarch's hand caught at Maya's sleeve, cool and steady, but Maya kept walking and the grip fell away.

Behind them, Dr. Richard was on his feet, reaching for his medical bag. "Eleanor. Whatever it takes. Call the yacht."

"I am not your operator, Richard. Call it yourself."

Maya's phone rang again as they reached the passage. This time, she answered.

"Detective Park?"

"Maya. I'm on the ferry. We'll be at the dock in twenty minutes. Bar Harbor patrol's behind me. What's happening?"

"Basement," Maya said, carrying the boy into the narrow passage. "Secret room behind the basement wall. Child victim, sedated. Richard's armed. Get a medic."

"Maya, what do you mean armed? What."

The line went dead as Dr. Richard yanked the phone from her hand and smashed it against the stone wall.

"You've just signed that boy's death warrant," he said, pulling another syringe from his bag. "And your own."

But Maya was already moving through the passage, James beside her, the boy's weight growing heavier in her arms. Behind them, she could hear Dr. Richard's footsteps. Too light. He was not following them. He was turning the other way.

"The service stairs," James said, breathless, understanding before she did. "He's going for the dock."

"Good. Less in our way."

"This way." James led her toward a passage that sloped upward. "There's an exit near the kitchen."

They emerged into the main house to find it eerily quiet. Through the windows, the gray afternoon light. In the distance, a boat. The Blackwood yacht, cutting away from the private dock toward the open water.

"No." James's voice broke. "No, he's not."

"He is." Maya laid the boy on the kitchen table, checking his pulse. Still steady, but his breathing was shallow. "He's gone. Let him go."

"But the police."

"Won't be here for fifteen minutes. Coast Guard won't be scrambled for thirty. By then that yacht's in open water with a hundred coves to hide in. Let him go."

"Maya."

"James, listen to me. That boy needs a medic. We stay with him. We give Park everything. Richard runs. Richard gets caught later." Her drawl was hard as the iron pan on the stove. "He doesn't get to take this kid with him."

James nodded, his eyes wet. "Oh God. I thought he was treating disturbed children. Helping them with therapy."

"Like he helped me. Like he helped Sarah."

James's face crumpled. "Sarah tried to tell me. In her last weeks she kept saying she remembered things from when she was little. About the other children. But Mother said it was impossible, and Richard said it was just the medication making her confused."

"The medication that was supposed to help her remember, not forget."

"Yes. She was getting better, remembering more. And then." James looked at the unconscious boy. "Oh God. He killed her because she was remembering."

A cold, small sound from the hallway. Eleanor, walking toward them unhurried, as though the afternoon had not just cracked itself open around her.

"James," Eleanor said. "Step away from that woman."

"No, Mother."

"I did not raise you to stand against your own family."

"You didn't raise me at all." James stayed where he was, one hand on the boy's chest to keep him steady. "You and Richard raised me to be afraid of you. You did it well. Sarah's dead because of it."

Eleanor's eyes moved to Maya. There was no crack in her. Only the narrow, cold calculation of a woman assessing a loss.

"I will not answer questions without my attorney," she said. "Neither will James. This is the only thing I will say to you, Ms. Chen."

"Then don't say it to me. Say it to Detective Park."

Eleanor did not answer. She turned and walked out of the kitchen, back into the library, and closed the door behind her.

Through the kitchen window, Maya watched the yacht's white wake shrink toward the horizon. Then, closer, the blunt grey bulk of the ferry rounding the headland.

"She's going to destroy evidence," James said.

"She's going to try. The library safe, the papers, whatever she can burn in the fireplace. Let her. Park has enough to hold her for questioning. The paper trail is backed up in eight places Richard doesn't know about."

"How do you know?"

"Because I've been doing this fifteen years and I back everything up. Now stay with the boy."

Maya did not follow Eleanor into the library. She stood at the kitchen sink and watched the ferry come in, and she tapped her right index finger against the rim of the stainless steel basin, and she counted until the boots hit the porch.

Detective Park came through the door first, weapon drawn, taking in the kitchen in one sweep. Her eyes landed on the boy, on James's hands steadying his chest, on Maya at the sink with the brass letter opener still in her left hand.

"Maya."

"He's sedated. He needs a medic now."

Park's hand went to her radio. Behind her, two Bar Harbor patrol officers moved into the house, one peeling off toward the library where Eleanor had gone, the other already kneeling by the boy. A paramedic was coming up the porch steps with a red case.

"Where's Blackwood?"

Maya pointed out the window. The wake was still faintly visible, white on gray.

"Southeast. Twenty minutes ago. The Blackwood yacht."

"God damn it." Park spoke fast into her radio. A Coast Guard acronym Maya didn't catch, coordinates, a description. Then she lowered the radio and looked at Maya and her voice dropped half a register.

"You okay, hon?"

Maya stared at her. Park's Atlanta Southern, almost buried under fifteen years of northern weather, had surfaced in a single word.

"Not really," Maya said. Her own drawl came back to meet it without asking. "No, ma'am. Not really at all."

Park's hand landed briefly on her shoulder, weighed nothing, lifted away.

"Then let's sit you down."

The paramedic had the boy on oxygen. James was on his knees beside the table, both hands on the boy's small still arm. Through the library door, the quiet sound of Eleanor saying something cold and correct to a patrol officer who was not answering.

Dr. Richard's yacht was a white speck and then a smaller white speck and then the horizon.

Maya sat down at the kitchen table and put her hand flat on the wood.

"His name's probably not going to be in any system," she said. "The boy. Richard wouldn't bring him in through anything traceable."

"We'll work it," Park said. "Mainland's waking up. We'll work it."

Outside, the paramedic's radio crackled. Inside, James kept his hands on the boy's chest and wept without making any sound at all.
